# Media Pitch & PR Outreach

## Overview

This skill produces concise, compelling media pitches that respect journalists' time and clearly communicate newsworthiness. It handles the full spectrum from research press pitches to op-ed proposals to podcast booking requests.

## When This Skill Triggers

- User says "I want to pitch this to a reporter" or "help me get media coverage"
- User asks to write an op-ed pitch, guest column proposal, or expert source pitch
- User wants to reach out to a journalist, editor, or podcast host
- User mentions media outreach, press coverage, or earned media strategy
- User has research findings and wants to translate them for public audiences

## Pitch Types

### 1. News Story Pitch
Proposing a story idea to a reporter or editor based on research, data, or a timely angle.

### 2. Expert Source Pitch
Offering yourself or someone else as an expert source for stories a journalist is already covering.

### 3. Op-Ed / Guest Column Pitch
Proposing a bylined opinion piece to an editor.

### 4. Podcast / Interview Pitch
Requesting a booking as a guest on a podcast, webinar, or broadcast segment.

### 5. Event / Announcement Pitch
Pitching coverage of a specific event, launch, or milestone.

## The Anatomy of a Good Pitch

### Subject Line
The subject line determines whether the email gets opened. Rules:
- Under 8 words
- Includes the news hook or "so what"
- No clickbait, no ALL CAPS, no exclamation marks
- Reads like a headline the journalist might actually write

**Good**: "New study: Solutions journalism boosts civic action by 40%"
**Bad**: "EXCITING Research Findings You Need to See!!!"
**Bad**: "Inquiry Regarding Potential Media Coverage Opportunity"

### Pitch Structure (150-250 words total)

```
[Subject line]

Hi [First name],

[Hook: 1-2 sentences. Why should they care RIGHT NOW?
Tie to a current news cycle, trend, or audience pain point.
Do not start with "I am writing to..." or "My name is..."]

[The substance: 2-3 sentences. What's the story?
Include the most compelling data point, finding, or angle.
Be specific: numbers, names, stakes.]

[Your credibility: 1-2 sentences. Why are you the right source?
Mention relevant credentials, publications, or experience.
Keep it brief; don't attach a full CV.]

[The ask: 1 sentence. What do you want?
"Would you be interested in covering this?"
"I'd be happy to provide an interview or additional data."
"I've attached a draft op-ed for your consideration."]

[Sign-off]
[Name, Title, Affiliation]
[Phone number for quick follow-up]
```

## Pitch Writing Rules

1. **Lead with the story, not yourself.** Journalists care about the story first, the source second.
2. **Answer "why now?"** Every pitch needs a timing hook. What makes this relevant today?
3. **One pitch, one idea.** Don't bundle multiple story angles into one email.
4. **Respect their beat.** Only pitch journalists who cover your topic. Reference their recent work if possible.
5. **Keep it scannable.** Short paragraphs, no attachments unless requested, no PDFs.
6. **Include a phone number.** Journalists on deadline need to reach you fast.
7. **No jargon.** Translate academic or technical language into plain English.
8. **No follow-up before 5 business days.** One polite follow-up is acceptable. Two is the max.

## Newsworthiness Assessment

Before pitching, evaluate whether the story has legs:

| Factor | Strong | Weak |
|--------|--------|------|
| Timeliness | Connects to active news cycle | No timing hook |
| Impact | Affects many people or a specific community deeply | Abstract or theoretical |
| Novelty | First of its kind, surprising finding, counterintuitive | Incremental or expected |
| Conflict/tension | Challenges conventional wisdom, reveals a problem | Confirms what's already known |
| Proximity | Relevant to the outlet's audience/geography | No local or audience connection |
| Human element | Real people affected, compelling narratives | Data-only, no human angle |

A strong pitch hits at least 3 of these factors.

## Pitch Variations by Type

### Research-to-Media Translation

Academic findings need significant translation for media pitches:
- Replace methodology details with findings and implications
- Lead with the "so what" not the "what we did"
- Translate statistical significance into plain-language impact
- Identify the human angle: who does this affect and how?

**Academic**: "A moderated mediation analysis revealed that solutions-oriented framing, mediated through positive affect (b = .12, 95% CI [.04, .21]), significantly predicted pro-environmental behavioral intentions."

**Pitch-ready**: "People who read solutions-focused environmental news were 40% more likely to take action on climate issues, compared to those who read problem-only coverage. The key? The stories made them feel hopeful, not just informed."

### Op-Ed Pitch

Op-ed pitches need:
- A clear thesis stated in one sentence
- Why you're the right person to write this (credentials + lived experience)
- A proposed word count (typically 700-900 words)
- Whether you've attached a draft or are proposing the idea first
- Exclusivity: note if you're pitching exclusively to this outlet

### Podcast Booking Pitch

Podcast pitches need:
- Reference a specific episode you've listened to (proves you know the show)
- 2-3 specific talking points, not vague expertise claims
- What makes you a good conversationalist (not just knowledgeable)
- Links to previous media appearances or talks (if available)

## Follow-Up Protocol

- Wait 5-7 business days before following up
- Follow-up should be 2-3 sentences max
- Add a new angle or timely hook if possible
- If no response after second follow-up, move on
- Never guilt-trip or express frustration about non-response

## Clarifying Questions

1. What's the story or finding you want to pitch?
2. Who is the target outlet or journalist? (If unsure, I can help identify appropriate contacts)
3. What's the timing hook? Why would this matter to readers right now?
4. Have you had any prior contact with this journalist?
5. Is this an exclusive pitch, or are you sending to multiple outlets?

## Hard Rules

- **Never misrepresent research findings** to make them sound more dramatic
- **Never pitch a journalist on a beat they don't cover**
- **Never send mass-blast pitches.** Every pitch should be personalized.
- **Always include a phone number.** This is non-negotiable for media outreach.
- **Never attach large files** without being asked. Link to resources instead.
