# Op-Ed Structure & Outlet Guidelines

Reference for structuring opinion pieces and pitching them to specific outlets. Update this file with notes from your own submission experiences.

---

## Op-Ed Anatomy

A strong op-ed follows a clear argumentative arc. Most outlets expect 700–900 words unless otherwise specified.

### Standard Structure

**1. The Hook (50–75 words)**
Open with a scene, a surprising fact, a counterintuitive claim, or a moment that captures the larger issue. Avoid starting with "I" or a broad historical overview. The first sentence must earn the reader's attention.

**2. The Thesis (1–2 sentences)**
State your argument directly. What do you believe, and why does it matter right now? This is the sentence the editor uses to decide if the piece is worth reading.

**3. Context and Stakes (150–200 words)**
Why does this issue matter, and why now? Provide the minimum necessary background without over-explaining. Assume an educated general reader, not an expert in your field.

**4. Your Evidence (250–350 words)**
Make your case. Use 2–3 concrete supporting points — data, research, examples, lived experience. Each point should build on the last. Avoid listing more than 3 arguments; depth beats breadth in opinion writing.

**5. Addressing the Counterargument (75–100 words)**
Acknowledge the strongest objection to your position and briefly refute it. Skipping this makes the piece feel one-sided. Engaging it shows intellectual honesty.

**6. The Call to Action or Resolution (75–100 words)**
What should happen? Who should do what? End with a specific, actionable conclusion — not "we must do better" but "Congress should / editors should / universities must." The closing sentence should have the same force as the opening.

## Pitch Format for Op-Eds

When pitching an op-ed idea (as opposed to submitting a finished draft):

- State your thesis in one sentence
- Explain why you are the right person to write this (credentials + perspective)
- Note your proposed word count
- Say whether you are pitching the idea or have a draft ready
- If pitching exclusively, say so explicitly (most outlets require exclusivity)

If you have a draft ready: attach it or paste it in the body of the email. Do not attach a PDF.

## Red Flags to Avoid

- **Stale timing**: If your news hook is more than 2 weeks old, reframe or wait for the next opportunity
- **Vague argument**: "We need to have a conversation about X" is not a thesis
- **Academic hedging**: "This suggests that perhaps..." weakens the argument for opinion writing
- **Jargon**: Translate methodology into findings; translate findings into impact
- **Missing credentials**: Editors need to know why you're the right person for this specific argument
